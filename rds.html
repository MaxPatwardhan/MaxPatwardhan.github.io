Haptic/Robot Telemanipulation System

Argonne National Laboratory and Northwestern University

Selective Graduate Capstone Project; January 2024 - May 2024

Background

This fast-paced project, developed as part of Northwestern University's selective capstone program in collaboration with Argonne National Laboratory, focused on designing and implementing a sophisticated haptic-robot telemanipulation system. Argonne National Laboratory desired a system that would allow their chemists to remotely work with radioactive materials, necessitating a highly precise-yet-strong haptic/robot system. Our solution enables users to control a robotic finger and thumb using a haptic exoskeleton. I drove the rapid, full-stack design and development of the project's electronics, ultimately providing a highly reliable, robust, and effortlessly integrable solution.

System Overview

Our team meticulously designed the mechanical, electrical, and software components of this project within just five months, ultimately developing a novel approach to human-robot devices that offers distinct advantages over existing systems. Unlike other haptic or robotic hand systems that struggle to accurately translate forces between the robot and the userâ€”often causing rigid objects grasped by the robot to feel squishy to the operatorâ€”our design ensures precise force feedback. This precision is crucial when operators need to work with delicate equipment to manipulate small objects.
Traditional power transfer solutions fall short in this application: gear-train systems exhibit noticeable backlash, linkages are overly complex and hinder a low-profile design, and pneumatic or hydraulic systems cannot match the force exerted by the human hand. To overcome these challenges, we chose a cable-driven design powered by brushless DC motors positioned near the operator's palm. This approach eliminates system play while maintaining a slender and robust mechanical structure. The use of cables enabled us to focus on a high-gain controller, providing operators with an accurate representation of the robotâ€™s interactions.
The system comprises distinct haptic and robotic components for both fingers and thumbs, each featuring two actuated degrees of freedom and a third passive degree of freedom. This configuration allows for natural and precise motion while ensuring the design remains feasible within the projectâ€™s timeframe.
I developed a modular electrical system for these robotic and haptic devices, creating three different PCBs to manage power, communication, and control for each subsystem. The 'power board' supplies and regulates power across four distinct domains, ensuring stable voltage with multiple user and system protections. The 'data board' supports real-time communication through a dual CAN bus architecture, controlled by a Teensy microcontroller and equipped with fault-tolerant transceivers for both intra-system and inter-system communication. High-resolution magnetic encoders deliver essential feedback for motion control, while integrated safety mechanisms, including emergency stops and system monitoring, protect both the operator and the hardware.

Bill of Materials

Mechanical System

Robot Finger

The robot finger subsystem serves as a mechanical analog to a human finger, capable of mimicking natural curl motions through its two actively actuated degrees of freedom. A pulley-based transmission system provides a 4:1 gear ratio at each joint, ensuring high torque and smooth motion. The system is powered by high-performance Maxon EC 45 flat motors, which deliver the torque required for precise manipulation while maintaining a compact form factor. The design prioritizes structural integrity, with components fabricated using a combination of water jet cutting and CNC milling techniques. Stainless steel cables serve as the primary transmission medium, tensioned using screws and nuts to ensure consistent performance under varying loads. Magnetic encoders are integrated for accurate positional feedback, enabling precise control of joint movements. While the subsystem demonstrates high torque efficiency and reliable performance, challenges persist in coupling the linkages and streamlining the assembly process. Future improvements could include integrating compact gearboxes or modular components to enhance assembly speed and reduce weight.

Robot Thumb

The robot thumb subsystem closely emulates human thumb kinematics, featuring two actuated degrees of freedom that enable waggle and curl motions. The waggle joint achieves a 7.33:1 transmission ratio using Maxon ECX Torque 22XL motors, ensuring smooth and powerful actuation. The coupled curl joints, operating at a 7.94:1 ratio, are actuated by Maxon EC 45 FLAT motors, known for their compact design and high torque efficiency. The subsystem utilizes a robust cable-driven mechanism with multiple wraps and tensioning blocks to maintain precise motion under high loads. Key components, including pulleys and tensioning assemblies, are CNC-machined for accuracy and durability. Manufacturing processes, such as turning and milling, are employed to achieve tight tolerances, ensuring reliable coupling between mechanical components. While the design achieves its functional goals, future improvements could focus on minimizing friction in the cable-driven mechanism and optimizing the coupling between waggle and curl motions to enhance overall efficiency and ease of assembly.

Haptic Finger

The haptic finger subsystem replicates the kinematics of a human finger, enabling two actively controlled degrees of freedomâ€”Metacarpophalangeal (MCP) and Proximal Interphalangeal (PIP)â€”while also accommodating one passive degree of freedom for natural motion. The design employs a robust cable-driven transmission system, where stainless steel cables are routed through precision pulleys to achieve smooth actuation. To maintain optimal tension, the cables are terminated using vented screws and wing nuts, offering both reliability and ease of maintenance. The motors selected for this subsystem are Maxon ECX Torque 22XL, chosen for their exceptional torque output, high efficiency, and compact design. Magnetic encoders provide real-time positional feedback, ensuring precise motion tracking for enhanced haptic interaction. The subsystem is designed for modularity and ease of repair, featuring CNC-machined aluminum components for durability and weight reduction. However, the routing of cables remains complex and can be further optimized to simplify assembly and maintenance procedures. Future iterations may explore integrating linear actuators to reduce the dependency on cable tensioning systems.

Haptic Thumb

The haptic thumb subsystem is designed to facilitate intricate thumb movements, including waggle (abduction/adduction) and curl (flexion/extension). These two degrees of freedom are actively actuated, while an additional passive degree of freedom enhances user comfort during operation. The curl motion leverages a cable-driven mechanism with multiple idler pulleys and vented screws to maintain cable tension. The waggle joint employs compact Maxon ECX motors for precise actuation and low inertia, optimizing performance for dynamic tasks. The modular thimble interface, made from flexible TPU material, accommodates a wide range of thumb sizes while ensuring a secure and comfortable fit for users. The subsystem is designed to be lightweight and ergonomic, with CNC-machined components that contribute to its durability and precision. However, cable friction during motion remains a challenge, necessitating further refinement in cable routing and pulley design. Future iterations may introduce additional active degrees of freedom to replicate the complex movements of the thumb tip, enhancing the subsystemâ€™s overall dexterity.
